Physics Prize

About the prize

The Nobel Prize in Physics is an annual prize awarded by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences to those who have made the most outstanding contributions to humanity in the field of physics. This is one of five Nobel Prizes established by the will of Alfred Nobel in 1895 and began to be awarded in 1901, the remaining prizes are the Nobel Prizes in Chemistry, Literature, Peace, and Physiology and Medicine. . Traditionally, physics is the first prize awarded during the Nobel Prize ceremony. German physicist Wilhelm Röntgen was the first recipient of the Nobel Prize in Physics in recognition of the extraordinary contributions he made to the discovery of X-rays. This award is administered by the Nobel Foundation and is considered the most prestigious award a scientist can receive in the field of physics. The prize was presented in Stockholm at an annual ceremony on December 10, the anniversary of Nobel's death. As of 2022, a total of 221 individuals have received the award.

    Nationality: America

    Award year: 1956, 1972

    Prize motivation: Research on semiconductors and find out the transistor effect and the study of superconductivity theory, commonly known as BCS theory

    John Bardeen (May 23, 1908 - January 30, 1991) was an American physicist and electrical engineer, who has twice won the Nobel Prize: first in 1956 for his invention. invented the transistor with William Shockley and Walter Brattain, the second in 1972 with work on the theory of convection superconductivity (BCS Theory) with two other scientists, Leon Neil Cooper and John Robert Schrieffer.

    William Lawrence Bragg

    Nationality: Australia

    Award year: 1915

    Prize motivation: Fabrication of an instrument for analyzing crystal structure by X-ray

    Sir William Lawrence Bragg Royal Society, (March 31, 1890 – July 1, 1971) was an Australian physicist. He was a co-recipient of the 1915 Nobel Prize in Physics with his father Sir William Henry Bragg for the invention and manufacture of an instrument for analyzing crystal structure using X-rays. As of 2018, he is the youngest person. He was a Nobel laureate, when he received the prize he was only 25 years old. He is also the director of Cavendish Laboratories, Cambridge.

    Marie Curie

    Nationality: Poland

    Award year: 1903

    Prize motivation: Research on radioactivity

    Maria Skłodowska – Marie Curie (7 November 1867 – 4 July 1934) was a Polish physicist and chemist. She is considered the first woman in the study of radioactivity when she coined the term for it. Marie is also the first woman to receive a Nobel Prize, the first and only woman to have the honor of winning two Nobel Prizes in two different fields – physics and chemistry. She was also the first female university lecturer at the University of Paris (Sorbonne). In 1995, her ashes were placed in memory at the Panthéon in Paris for her great contributions to humanity and the world.

    Kajita Takaaki

    Nationality: Japan

    Award year: 2015

    Prize motivation: Discovering oscillating neutrinos and proving they have mass

    Kajita Takaaki (梶田隆章, born 1959) is a Japanese physicist, best known for the neutrino experiment at Kamiokande and its successor Super-Kamiokande. In 2015, he received the Nobel Prize in physics along with Arthur B. McDonald. The Nobel committee awarded the two scientists the prize for "their substantial contributions to the tests showing that the neutrino (a type of subatomic particle produced by the decay of radioactive elements) This transformation requires neutrinos to have mass." "This discovery has changed our understanding of the innermost workings of matter and could prove crucial to our view of the universe." Kajita was born in 1959 in Higashimatsuyama, Saitama.[1] He is married to Michiko who lives in Toyama.

    David Gross

    Nationality: America

    Award year: 2004

    Prize motivation: Finding the asymptotic degrees of freedom in the strong interaction

    David Jonathan Gross (born February 19, 1941) is an American theoretical theorist and string theorist. Together with Frank Wilczek and David Politzer, he was awarded the 2004 Nobel Prize in Physics for discovering the freedom of myopia. He is the former director and current chair of the Frederick W. Gluck Chair in Theoretical Physics at the Kavli Institute for Theoretical Physics at the University of California, Santa Barbara. He is also a faculty member in the UC Santa Barbara Department of Physics and is currently a Fellow of the Chapman University Quantum Research Institute in California. He is a foreign member of the Chinese Academy of Sciences.
